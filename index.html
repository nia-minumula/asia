Why "Chicagoland," specifically

Most intro material treats Asian America as a coastal story — San Francisco, Los Angeles, New York. Chicago's story is quieter but just as layered: a South Side Chinatown that has anchored Chinese Chicago since the early 1900s, a Vietnamese and pan-Asian corridor that grew up around the Argyle Red Line stop, and a Devon Avenue strip in West Ridge that became one of the country's most concentrated South Asian commercial districts starting in the 1980s.

None of these neighborhoods are museum pieces. They're where people shop for groceries, open restaurants, hold parades, and organize politically — which is exactly why they're a better classroom than a textbook chapter.

This site doesn't replace a real course. Think of it as the syllabus you'd want before the first day: a sense of what's out there, who's writing and filming and organizing, and which local campuses take the field seriously.
